Con Dao sits 230 kilometres south of Ho Chi Minh City in the South China Sea, and the forty-minute flight deposits you in a completely different Vietnam from anything on the mainland. The airport is barely more than a runway. The main town has one market, one road along the waterfront, and a prison complex that occupies a full block and changed the course of Vietnamese history.

That prison is where you should begin. The Con Dao Museum and the adjacent Phu Hai Prison camp are genuinely difficult to move through — the conditions documented here, the scale of the suffering, and the names of the dead etched into the walls create a moral weight that colours everything that follows. The Tiger Cages — concrete pits barely tall enough to stand — are the most disturbing historical site I've encountered in Southeast Asia. Coming here before you see the beaches is the right order.

The beaches absorb that weight slowly. Dat Doc Beach is one kilometre of fine white sand with the South China Sea on three sides and national park forest on the fourth. There is no development here — no beach bars, no sunbed operators — just the water and the trees. The snorkelling off the headland reveals live coral in remarkable health, the kind you used to find everywhere along Southeast Asia's coasts before mass tourism and bleaching events arrived.

Bay Canh Island is the spiritual heart of the national park. The boat ride takes forty minutes from Con Son pier, passing through waters so clear you can see the reef twenty metres down. On the island's eastern beach, green sea turtles have nested every year since long before Vietnam existed as a nation. Watching a turtle emerge from the ocean in the middle of the night to lay her eggs in the sand above the tide line — by moonlight, in complete silence, in a place that has barely changed in a thousand years — is one of the most affecting experiences travelling has given me.

Con Dao doesn't require much. It requires only that you slow down and let the history and the nature work on you in their own time.
