Ha Giang is the destination that serious Vietnam travellers save for last — not because it's the hardest to reach, but because it sets a standard that makes everywhere else feel slightly ordinary by comparison.

The Dong Van Loop doesn't announce itself gradually. You're riding through farmland north of Ha Giang city and then, without warning, the road tilts upward and the limestone peaks close in from both sides. By the time you crest Quan Ba Pass and look down into the Twin Mountains valley for the first time, you understand why people come back to this corner of Vietnam again and again.

The Mã Pí Lèng Pass is the centrepiece — a 20-kilometre stretch carved into a cliff face above the Nho Que River gorge, dropping over 1,000 metres in a single unbroken sweep. There's a viewpoint halfway that no photograph fully captures. The turquoise river is so far below it looks painted. The road ahead curves around the canyon wall and disappears into mountain haze.

The Hmong villages of the plateau — Dong Van, Lung Cu, Pho Cao — are places where traditional life is still intact in ways that the more accessible minority areas of the north have lost. The Sunday Dong Van Market is the real thing: Hmong women in embroidered indigo dress, corn wine sold from plastic jugs, livestock traders, silver jewellery laid on cloth. Arrive before 9am to see it at its most authentic.

Come in October for the harvest, when the terraces above Hoang Su Phi turn gold and the entire plateau smells of rice and woodsmoke. Or come in March for the buckwheat flowers — small pink blooms that cover the limestone fields and give Ha Giang a completely different mood, softer and more intimate than the dramatic autumn version.

Either way, give it five days. The loop will teach you patience and then reward it.
